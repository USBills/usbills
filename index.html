May 12th, 2026 to May 15th, 2026

H.R. 5625 (Cashless Bail Reporting Act) is a bill that directs the acting Attorney General and the Department of Justice to publish a list of state and local governments that use cashless bail for individuals charged with crimes. It specifically targets two crimes: any violent or sexual act (ex. murder, rape, sexual battery, burglary, carjacking, robbery, assault...) and any offense that promotes public disorder (ex. looting, vandalism, rioting, fleeing from law enforcement...). The bill increases transparency and federal oversight for local justice systems by allowing the federal government to monitor local policies. This bill benefits law enforcement and proponents of stricter bail reform by identifying jurisdictions with lax bail policies. It also benefits the public by providing a list of where local governments are implementing cashless bail for violent offenses.

H.R. 6260 (Keeping Violent Offenders Off Our Streets Act of 2025) is a bill that focuses on restricting charitable bail entities by classifying them as insurance entities. The bill aims to restrict the use of cashless bail, make it a federal crime for bail organizations to engage in fraud or misappropriation of funds, and require state licensing requirements by the state insurance commissions. This bill benefits the public by increasing accountability through federal oversight, along with the bail bond industry, by ensuring that charitable bail organizations are subject to the same regulatory framework.

H.R. 8365 (Monitor Accountability Act) is a bill that reforms court-appointed monitors (individuals appointed by judges to ensure that state or local government agencies comply with court orders). The bill enforces a term limit for monitors to serve only one term for 5 years, establishes a cap on fees that monitors can charge on local governments, mandates that cases must be transferred to a new judge after 6 years, and requires annual public accounting of monitor fees. The bill benefits local governments, non-federal law enforcement, and republican legislators by giving more autonomy to local governments that are trapped under constant federal decrees and reducing costs on taxpayers by limiting expensive judicial overreach by monitors.

H.R. 8469 (Military Construction, Veterans Affairs, and Related Agencies Appropriations Act, 2027) is a bill that funds military infrastructure and VA services for the upcoming fiscal year. The bill outlines billions of dollars, along with $157 billion in discretionary funding, for services that include VA medical services, veteran benefits, and military construction projects. The bill also prohibits funds for the closing of Guantanamo Bay, restricts the VA from reporting veterans to the National Instant Criminal Background Check Systems without a judicial order, and mandates the VA to stop using Social Security numbers for authentication in a push for modernization. The bill benefits veterans, defense contractors, and military personnel along with their families, by providing continued funding for their services.

H. Con. Res. 96 is a resolution that focuses on recognizing the importance of all law enforcement officers in the United States for their work and sacrifice. It recognizes them as indispensable to the functioning of the nation and serves as a symbolic gesture of solidarity. The resolution benefits law enforcement officers by providing formal backing for their continued funding and support.
